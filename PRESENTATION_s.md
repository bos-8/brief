## Slide 01 · Governance Framework

All of us already use AI in daily life.

But what happens when AI becomes part of systems connected to the human body?

That is why we propose the *Codex of Augmented Humanity* as a rights-based governance framework.

## Slide 02 · Regulatory Gap

The EU already has strong regulation, including the GDPR, the AI Act, and medical-device rules.

The problem is not the absence of law, but fragmentation.

AI-enabled augmentation combines body, data, software, and infrastructure in one system, and no single framework fully covers that.

## Slide 03 · Architectural Shift

AI does not just improve intervention; it changes its structure.

Traditional intervention is bounded and supervised, while AI-enabled enhancement can be continuous, adaptive, and connected.

Because of that, the core risk shifts from simple malfunction to dependency and power asymmetry.

## Slide 04 · Feedback Loop

AI-enabled augmentation works through a continuous loop: sensing, AI processing, actuation, and oversight.

The key point is that risk does not come from one element alone. It emerges from the interaction between data, decision, and bodily action.

## Slide 05 · Framework Blindspots

Current regulation often addresses separate components, not the full long-term relationship between a person and an adaptive augmentation system.

This leaves blind spots related to dependency, hidden control, and contractual lock-in.

No one should depend on a subscription to walk or even to breathe.

These are not minor technical issues, they directly affect autonomy and dignity.

## Slide 06 · Threat Analysis

Traditional safety analysis asks whether a device can fail.

In this context, we must also ask whether a system can coerce, discriminate, or create dependency.

That is why threat analysis must include not only technical failure, but also social, economic, and political risk.

## Slide 07 · Militarization

Enhancement can become attractive in military or security environments because it may improve resilience, recovery, or perception.

In hierarchical systems, even indirect pressure can make consent meaningless. That is why coercive enhancement must be prohibited.

## Slide 08 · Cyber-Physical Risks

A connected implant is not only a medical device, it is also a cyber-physical system.

This means digital vulnerabilities can translate directly into bodily harm.

For that reason, life-critical implants should not be remotely controllable by default.

## Slide 09 · Biological Harm

AI can accelerate biological design, simulation, and optimization.

While this can support medicine, it can also lower the barrier for harmful or targeted misuse.

That is why the Codex draws a strict red line against enhancement-related biological work intended to harm or persecute.

## Slide 10 · Social Sorting

Enhancement systems generate highly sensitive data, including health, biometric, genetic, and behavioral information.

AI can turn this data into predictions and scoring systems, creating the risk of hidden social sorting.

That is why enhancement-based discrimination must be strictly prohibited.

## Slide 11 · Economic Coercion

Economic coercion appears when bodily function depends on proprietary infrastructure, updates, or vendor support.

In that situation, market leverage becomes leverage over the body itself.

The Codex therefore requires continuity guarantees, transparency, and credible exit mechanisms.

## Slide 12 · Normalization Pressure

Not all pressure is direct, some of it comes from normalization.

What begins as optional enhancement may gradually become expected in sport, work, or other competitive settings.

Governance must therefore protect fairness, not only safety.

Like the structure in the image, an enhancement system may appear stable on the surface, but if the human being is removed from its central role, the entire normative structure begins to collapse.

## Slide 13 · Operational Constraints

The Codex is meant to be practical, not only theoretical.

It translates rights into concrete constraints, such as meaningful consent, fail-safe design, dual-use review, anti-discrimination, and continuity obligations.

In other words, rights must become operational requirements.

## Slide 14 · Continuous Governance

If the system is continuous, governance must also be continuous.

One-time approval is not enough for technologies that can update, evolve, and connect to new infrastructures over time.

That is why governance must include design-time limits, lifecycle obligations, and institutional enforcement.

## Slide 15 · Human Dignity

At the center of this discussion is human dignity.

AI-assisted augmentation can bring real benefits, but only if autonomy, equality, and bodily integrity are built into the architecture itself.

Otherwise, the system may begin to serve institutions or markets before it serves the person.

## Slide 16 · Final Question

We began with AI around us, but AI-enabled augmentation brings the issue much closer.

The real question is no longer only what AI helps us do, but who remains in control when AI becomes part of the body.

When AI enters the body, who truly stays in control: the human being, or the system around them?
