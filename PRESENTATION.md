## Slide 01 · Governance Framework

Today, all of us use AI.

We use it when we search.
When we translate.
When we navigate.
When we automate tasks.
AI is already around us.

But this presentation asks a different question.
What happens when AI is no longer only around us?
What happens when it becomes part of systems connected to the human body?

That is where human enhancement begins to change.

Our argument is simple: AI is turning enhancement from isolated medical intervention into something continuous, connected, and deeply embedded in larger systems.

And that means governance must change too.

So we propose the **Codex of Augmented Humanity**.
A rights-based framework built on dignity, bodily integrity, autonomy, data protection, and non-discrimination.

Because enhancement should serve the human being.
Not the system around them.

---

## Slide 02 · Regulatory Gap

The problem is not that Europe has no rules.

It does.

We already have GDPR.
We already have the AI Act.
We already have medical-device regulation.

These are strong frameworks.
But they mostly work side by side.

And that becomes a problem when augmentation is no longer just a device, or just software, or just data processing.

AI-enabled enhancement combines all of these at once.
It connects the body, the algorithm, the infrastructure, and the market.

So the gap is not a lack of law.
The gap is fragmentation.

And when governance is fragmented, control can also become fragmented.
That is where serious risks begin to appear.

---

## Slide 03 · Architectural Shift

AI does not just improve intervention.

It changes its architecture.

In the traditional medical model, intervention is usually limited.
It is supervised.
It is bounded in time.
And the parameters are mostly predefined.

But AI-enabled enhancement works differently.

It can be continuous.
It can adapt over time.
It can rely on external infrastructure.
And it can keep learning or optimizing.

So the central risk changes too.

The issue is no longer only malfunction.
It becomes dependency.
It becomes power asymmetry.
It becomes the question of who actually controls the system.

And once that happens, we are no longer dealing with a simple tool.
We are dealing with a socio-technical architecture that can shape bodily function over time.

---

## Slide 04 · Feedback Loop

This slide shows the logic of the system.

First, there is the **sensing layer**.
It collects data from the body.
Physiological data.
Behavioral data.
Device telemetry.

Second, there is the **AI layer**.
It processes that data.
It predicts.
It classifies.
It optimizes.

Third, there is the **actuation layer**.
This is where the system changes something.
It adjusts treatment.
It modifies device behavior.
It changes recommendations in real time.

And then there is the **oversight layer**.
Logging.
Validation.
Safety limits.
Compliance.

The important point is this: risk does not sit in one place.
It emerges from the loop itself.
From the continuous interaction between data, decision, and bodily action.

---

## Slide 05 · Framework Blindspots

Current regulation still leaves blindspots.

Why?

Because it often regulates a component.
Not the whole relationship between a person and an adaptive, connected augmentation system.

And that relationship may last for years.
Maybe for life.

The blindspots are especially clear in four areas:
long-term dependency,
continuous optimization,
hidden control,
and contractual lock-in.

These are not technical details.

They affect autonomy.
They affect dignity.
They affect what it means to remain in control of your own body.

So our position is very clear:
enhancement must never become a channel for exploitation.

That is exactly why a broader governance layer is needed.

---

## Slide 06 · Threat Analysis

Traditional safety analysis asks a reasonable question:
Can the device fail?

But here, that is not enough.

We also need to ask:
Can the system pressure people?
Can it discriminate?
Can it create dependency?
Can it quietly shift control away from the user?

AI changes the structure of risk.

It brings acceleration.
It scales decisions faster.

And it brings opacity.
It can become harder to understand, harder to challenge, and sometimes harder even to notice.

So threat analysis must move beyond technical failure.

It must include hierarchy.
Market power.
Competition.
Institutional pressure.
Strategic misuse.

In other words: the risk is not only technical.
It is also social, economic, and political.

---

## Slide 07 · Militarization

One of the clearest risks is militarization.

Once enhancement is linked to resilience, perception, or faster recovery, it may quickly become attractive in military or security settings.

And in hierarchical systems, pressure does not always need to be explicit.

Nobody has to say, “You must do this.”

It is enough that refusal affects deployability.
Or promotion.
Or status.
Or trust.

Then consent may still exist on paper.
But not in reality.

AI makes this stronger because it enables constant optimization and performance monitoring.

So our answer is straightforward.

Coercive enhancement must be prohibited.
Consent must remain meaningful, even in command structures.
And where interventions are irreversible, long-term medical support must be guaranteed.

---

## Slide 08 · Cyber-Physical Risks

A connected implant is not only a medical device.

It is also a cyber-physical system.

That means it inherits digital vulnerabilities.
But those vulnerabilities do not stay digital.

They reach the body.

A compromised implant is not like a hacked app.
It can directly affect human health and safety.

A connected insulin pump is a good example.
If communication or configuration is manipulated, the consequences can become physically dangerous very quickly.

There is also another layer of risk.
Remote control may shift practical power toward vendors or service providers.

So the governance principle here is strong:
life-critical implants should not be remotely controllable by default.

And when connectivity is necessary, it must be minimal, secure, auditable, and backed by a safe offline mode.

---

## Slide 09 · Biological Harm

This is the most severe misuse scenario.

AI can accelerate biological design.
It can improve modeling.
It can improve simulation.
It can optimize parameters faster than before.

In medicine, that can be beneficial.

But the same tools can also lower the barrier for harmful use.

And when those tools are combined with genetic data, biomarker data, or population-level information, the risk becomes even more serious.

So this is not only a technical issue.
It is a governance issue.
And in some cases, a security issue.

That is why the Codex draws a hard red line.

Enhancement-related biological work must be banned when the goal is harm, persecution, or population-targeted abuse.

And where AI materially accelerates biological scaling, strong dual-use review is necessary.

---

## Slide 10 · Social Sorting

Enhancement systems create data.

Very intimate data.

Health data.
Biometric data.
Genetic data.
Behavioral data.

And AI can turn that data into profiles, scores, and predictions.

That creates the risk of hidden social sorting.

A person may no longer be judged only by what they do.
They may be judged by what systems predict about them.

An employer may infer resilience.
An insurer may infer future cost.
A platform may infer capability or risk.

And slowly, access to opportunity may become linked to biological metrics.

That is why this is not just a privacy issue.
It is also an equality issue.

So the Codex supports a strict ban on enhancement-based discrimination and strong purpose limitation for all such data.

---

## Slide 11 · Economic Coercion

Economic coercion appears when bodily function depends on commercial infrastructure.

At that point, the market relationship changes.

It is no longer just about buying a product.
It becomes a question of continuity, dependence, and survival.

If a company controls updates, compatibility, support, or access to essential features, then market leverage can become leverage over the human body.

That is a serious ethical and legal problem.

Because a person should never be trapped in a system where essential bodily function depends on payment power or vendor obedience.

So the Codex rejects exploitative design patterns.
It requires continuity obligations.
It requires transparency about long-term support.
And it requires credible exit or escrow mechanisms.

Because no one should live inside a pay-to-live model.

---

## Slide 12 · Normalization Pressure

Not every threat comes from attack or direct coercion.

Some threats come from normalization.

What begins as optional enhancement may slowly become expected enhancement.

This is easy to imagine in sport.
But it can also happen in work, education, or competitive institutions.

Once enhancement shifts the baseline, others may feel they must upgrade just to keep up.

And that means pressure can emerge without any legal obligation at all.

The system itself creates the pressure.

So governance must address fairness, not only safety.

That is why the Codex calls for transparent eligibility rules, clear participation standards where needed, and protection against coercive enhancement through competition.

At the same time, therapeutic interventions must not become a reason for unfair exclusion.

---

## Slide 13 · Operational Constraints

This slide brings the argument down to practice.

The Codex is not meant to remain abstract.

It translates rights into constraints.

Concrete constraints.

For militarization, that means real consent and a ban on coercion.
For biological misuse, it means prohibition and dual-use review.
For cyber-physical risk, it means limiting remote control and requiring fail-safe design.
For data misuse, it means purpose limitation and anti-discrimination.
For economic dependency, it means continuity guarantees and protection against exploitative control.

So the key point is this:

Rights must become technical and institutional requirements.

Because if dignity and autonomy remain only general principles, they will be too weak against highly adaptive and connected systems.

---

## Slide 14 · Continuous Governance

If the system is continuous, governance must also be continuous.

That is the central idea here.

It is not enough to approve a system once and assume it stays safe forever.

AI-enabled augmentation can evolve.
It can update.
It can connect to new infrastructure.
It can change how it behaves over time.

So we propose governance at three levels.

First, **design-time constraints**.
Dangerous capabilities should be limited from the beginning.

Second, **lifecycle obligations**.
That includes updates, auditability, logging, and safe degraded modes.

Third, **institutional enforcement**.
Certification.
Audits.
Oversight.

This matters because risk is distributed.
So governance must be distributed too.

---

## Slide 15 · Human Dignity

At the center of this whole discussion is one principle: human dignity.

AI-assisted augmentation can bring real benefits.
It can restore health.
It can increase independence.
It can reduce suffering.
It can improve quality of life.

So this is not an anti-technology argument.

It is a pro-human argument.

Because once AI becomes integrated with the body, dignity cannot remain only a moral slogan.

It must become part of the architecture.

If autonomy, equality, and bodily integrity are not designed into the system, then the system may begin to serve institutional efficiency, commercial logic, or strategic interests before it serves the person.

And that is exactly what the Codex is meant to prevent.

---

## Slide 16 · Final Question

We started with something familiar.

All of us already live with AI.

But AI-enabled augmentation changes the depth of the question.

Now the issue is not only what AI helps us do.

The issue is what happens when AI becomes part of the systems that shape bodily function, personal autonomy, and human dependence.

And that leads us to one final question.

**When AI enters the body, who truly stays in control: the human being, or the system around them?**

That is the real issue.

Not only capability.
Not only innovation.
But power.

Because if rights are built into the architecture, augmentation can serve the person.

If they are not, control may slowly move elsewhere.
